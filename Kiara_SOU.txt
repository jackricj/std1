Connective tissues are integral to the support, protection, and integration of different bodily components. The three general characteristics of connective tissues are:

1. Specialized Cells: Connective tissues contain cells that are specifically designed to support and connect other types of cells and tissue within the body. Some types of these cells are fibroblasts, adipocytes, and leukocytes.

2. Extracellular Matrix: Connective tissues have a substantial amount of extracellular material or matrix. This matrix is created by the connective tissue cells and contains ground substance (non-fibrous protein and other molecules) and fibers (elastic, collagen, and reticular).

3. Degree of Vascularity: Connective tissues have varying degrees of vascularity. For instance, tissues such as cartilage are avascular, meaning they don't contain blood vessels. Other connective tissues, like adipose tissues, have an abundant supply of blood vessels. This allows for different rates of healing; avascular tissues heal at a much slower rate since they lack the blood vessels necessary for delivering oxygen and nutrients to the cells.
